We thank the reviewer for the careful reading. We agree that the core issue is scope and positioning: the current text can read as a broad alternative to online CP, whereas our exact guarantees are narrower. They use the batch-sequential model with conditional within-batch exchangeability and \(p\)-value threshold policies. Proposition 2.7 gives independence of true-label conformal \(p\)-values across batches; it is not a general online/adaptive CP theorem. We will state this prominently in the setup and before the main propositions. The one-test-point notation is for clarity. Multiple test points per batch can be handled by thresholding a valid batch-level/conformal \(p\)-value or aggregation, but the needed super-uniformity and independence must be stated for those batch-level quantities; we will avoid implying it is automatic.

We also agree that the comparison with e-processes should be framed as a tradeoff. E-process methods target stronger/worst-case anytime objectives and are appropriate when that is the desired guarantee. Our method exploits additional independence in the batch-sequential setting to target mask, restart, and restricted-closed-loop guarantees without past test labels. Thus efficiency differences are guarantee-specific, not universal superiority. We will soften the language and present Section 5 as a cautionary Gamma calculation for squared-loss conformal scores, not as a broad verdict that e-processes collapse in realistic settings.

The optimization should also be more concrete. The cost \(c_t(\tau)\) is a proxy for expected set size or interval width at threshold \(\tau\), estimated from calibration scores; the forecast affects efficiency only. Validity depends solely on satisfying the log-budget constraints \(\sum_t M_t s_t \le L\). We will add a mask-family example: for \(K\)-window masks, the constraints are \(\sum_{u=j}^{j+K-1} s_u \le L\) for \(j=1,\ldots,T-K+1\), with singleton and all-ones masks recovering marginal and anytime extremes. This also clarifies how Bonferroni, uniform-horizon schedules, and classical alpha-spending are feasible schedules within the same threshold-allocation class.
