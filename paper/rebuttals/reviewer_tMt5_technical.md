We thank the reviewer for the constructive comments. The main clarification is the information model: our assumptions are local to each batch, not a stationarity assumption over time.

At batch \(t\), the deployment stream may contain a small labeled calibration sample \(\mathcal S^t\) and unlabeled operational test points. The labels in \(\mathcal S^t\) are available for conformal calibration; the test labels are exactly the labels to be covered and may be delayed, expensive, or unavailable before later decisions. Conditional on the past \(\mathcal F_{t-1}\), we assume only within-batch exchangeability between \(\mathcal S^t\) and the test point. Cross-batch drift is allowed through dependence on \(\mathcal F_{t-1}\). This is complementary to CP under distribution shift or non-exchangeability, which relaxes a different assumption and often gives frequency-style guarantees; our target is finite-sample control of the probability of any error in specified windows.

Restart validity is also a property of the procedure, not a new data assumption. It rules out algorithms that spend all their validity before the first monitored failure and then become vacuous. Formally, after a monitored error, the remaining procedure must still control the next monitored error as if monitoring restarted.

For Section 4, validity does not depend on solving a difficult optimization exactly. The log transform \(s_t=-\log(1-\tau_t)\) turns each mask constraint into the linear budget constraint \(\sum_t M_t s_t\le -\log(1-\alpha)\). The cost functions choose among valid schedules. In applications they can be expected set size or interval width estimated from calibration scores; inaccurate forecasts affect efficiency and the suboptimality bound, not coverage or restart validity, because the budget constraints are enforced at every replan.
